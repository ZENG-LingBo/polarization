\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

Our project asks how to \emph{defuse} affective polarization between rival fan
communities---specifically Los Angeles Lakers and Boston Celtics
supporters---within a simulated social-media platform, using a population of
generative-AI agents that exert peer-level \emph{social influence} rather than
top-down correction. We choose an identity-based sports rivalry because it is a
tractable, ecologically familiar instance of affective polarization whose
mechanisms are domain-general: an intervention that works here speaks to
affective polarization more broadly. We situate the project along three lines.
\emph{Sociologically} (\S\ref{sec:rw-socio}), we frame polarization as a
group-level, identity-driven structural phenomenon. \emph{Psychologically}
(\S\ref{sec:rw-psych}), we identify the intergroup mechanisms that sustain it
and the points where an intervention can cut in. \emph{Technically}
(\S\ref{sec:rw-tech}), we review how LLM and multi-agent systems can carry that
intervention inside a social platform. Table~\ref{tab:rw} positions
representative prior \emph{systems} against our design commitments.

\subsection{Sociological: Polarization as a Group-Level, Identity-Driven Phenomenon}
\label{sec:rw-socio}

Contemporary polarization is increasingly understood as \emph{affective} rather
than merely issue-based: people feel warmth toward their in-group and animus
toward the out-group, independent of how far apart their actual positions
are~\cite{iyengar2015fear,iyengar2019origins}. This animus is rooted in group
identity. Social Identity Theory holds that simply categorizing oneself into a
group generates in-group favoritism and out-group derogation, so conflict is
driven by identity rather than by tangible stakes~\cite{tajfel1979sit}. Sports
fandom is a paradigmatic case: degree of identification with a team is a genuine
social identity that structures perception and behavior~\cite{wann1993identification},
which is why a historic, city-based rivalry such as Lakers versus Celtics
functions as identity-based polarization rather than a mere difference of
preference---and a cleaner model than player-versus-player disputes.

On everyday platforms, this identity-driven antagonism plays out as toxic,
self-reinforcing discourse. Our own study of a live online forum shows rival fans
recruiting generative AI to sharpen their arguments and escalating as opposing
newcomers arrive~\cite{zeng2025ronaldo}, while our work on how people express
contested opinions and emotions on TikTok~\cite{zhang2026climate} and build
identity-laden communities on Instagram~\cite{zhang2026adhd} and online-dating
platforms~\cite{zhang2025dating} documents the affective, performative character
of online opinion expression. Critically for our method, Donkers and
Ziegler~\cite{donkers2025synthetic} demonstrate that a \emph{synthetic} social
network populated by LLM agents can reproduce the key dynamics of polarized
discourse under controlled, manipulable conditions---establishing both the
construct validity of a simulated platform and a methodological precedent for the
environment we build. Together these works establish the problem space: rival
identities produce affective polarization that is enacted and amplified online,
motivating an intervention sited in the medium itself.

\subsection{Psychological: Intergroup Mechanisms and Where Intervention Cuts In}
\label{sec:rw-psych}

If polarization is sustained by social categorization, decades of intergroup
research specify how to loosen it. The Contact Hypothesis, confirmed across
hundreds of studies, shows that contact between groups reduces prejudice under
supportive conditions~\cite{pettigrew2006contact}; the Robbers Cave studies show
that \emph{superordinate cooperative goals}---tasks that require the groups to
work together---reduce conflict more durably than contact
alone~\cite{sherif1961robbers}. The Common Ingroup Identity Model (CIIM)
explains why: recategorizing former out-group members into a shared, more
inclusive in-group (``we are all NBA fans'') redirects in-group favoritism
toward the erstwhile rival, reducing bias~\cite{gaertner2000reducing}. This
mechanism has direct empirical support in our exact domain: Levine et
al.~\cite{levine2005identity} found that football fans helped an injured rival
supporter far more often when a superordinate ``football fan'' identity, rather
than a narrow team identity, was salient. This is the theoretical core of our
design---and it carries two cautions. First, recategorization that erases
subgroup identities can trigger distinctiveness threat, so a \emph{dual-identity}
framing that preserves team identity while adding the superordinate one is
typically more robust; and a shared cooperative goal is preferable to a common
\emph{enemy}, which tends to displace hostility rather than dissolve it.

The second caution concerns \emph{how} an intervention is delivered. Psychological
Reactance Theory holds that perceived attempts to constrain one's freedom---
including overt persuasion---provoke resistance and a rebound toward the
threatened position~\cite{brehm1966reactance}. Recent HCI evidence sharpens this
for AI: when an AI simply agrees with or validates a user's existing stance,
confidence in the polarized position \emph{increases} and depolarization becomes
less likely~\cite{whenaiagrees2026}. Heavy-handed, content-level persuasion by an
LLM is therefore the wrong lever. This motivates our commitment to
\emph{structural} rather than rhetorical intervention: arranging the platform so
that participants \emph{discover} a common identity through cooperative
cross-group interaction, with agents shaping the social context rather than
arguing positions.

\subsection{Technical: Multi-Agent LLM Social Influence in Simulated Platforms}
\label{sec:rw-tech}

\textbf{(Provisional---to be finalized once the system design is locked.)}
Realizing a structural intervention requires agents that can populate and shape a
live platform. Generative agents can credibly simulate human memory, reflection,
and social action at scale~\cite{park2023generative}, and our own systems show
this is achievable for influence: multimodal agents can prompt people's actions
and shift behavior~\cite{zhang2025ecoecho}, and LLM agents can simulate a
reactive public whose opinion moves believably~\cite{tang2025breaking}. AI
mediators can measurably depolarize online debate, though their effect depends on
social positioning and conversational style rather than on argument
alone~\cite{govers2024aimediators}.

Our design leans on \emph{multiple} agents as the active ingredient. Rather than a
single authoritative mediator, a population of peer-like agents can \emph{model}
de-escalated behavior for participants to imitate---showing both sides in
respectful exchange, or enacting cross-rival harmony that establishes a descriptive
norm. Agent \emph{turn-taking} can itself be a lever: visibly alternating, inclusive
participation signals that ``everyone talks,'' nudging the group away from
pile-on dynamics. The conversational competencies our group has developed support
this---context-aware pacing and active listening govern when and how an agent
should speak to seem human and earn standing~\cite{jiang2026hear}---as do
low-reactance, playful framings of cooperative tasks, in the register of our
LLM-driven games for attitude change~\cite{zhou2024eternagram,du2025invisible}.
Finally, because agents will be steered to model moderate, prosocial positions,
how LLMs encode moral and attitudinal stances is a live design concern that our
analysis of model moral responses begins to map~\cite{hui2025moral}.

\paragraph{Summary and gap.}
The sociological literature establishes that rival identities produce affective
polarization enacted online (\S\ref{sec:rw-socio}); the psychological literature
specifies recategorization toward a common in-group as a defusing mechanism, while
warning that overt AI persuasion backfires (\S\ref{sec:rw-psych}); and the
technical literature shows LLM multi-agent systems can simulate and influence
social environments (\S\ref{sec:rw-tech}). As Table~\ref{tab:rw} shows, no prior
system unites these: a \emph{simulated social platform} that recruits an
\emph{already-polarized} rivalry and uses a \emph{multi-agent} community of GenAI
peers to defuse affective polarization \emph{structurally}---through cooperative
recategorization and behavioral modeling rather than direct persuasion. Our
project occupies precisely this gap.
