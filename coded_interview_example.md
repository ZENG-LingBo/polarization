# Worked Coded-Interview Example (reference for coders)

A **mock** post-session interview (Lakers/Celtics study) taken end-to-end through the
lab pipeline: **transcript → Level 1 open coding → initial-coding notes → Level 2 focused
coding → theme + subthemes → results-paragraph**. Use it as the gold-standard format
reference; it is fabricated for illustration, not real data.

Participant: **P7**, Celtics fan, high SSIS, intervention condition.

---

## 1. Transcript (excerpt — lab format: alternating Q/A, no speaker labels)

> What was that discussion like for you?
>
> Honestly? A dumpster fire at the start. I posted one thing about Tatum and immediately
> three Lakers guys piled on, just "ratio" and clown emojis. I almost closed the tab.
>
> What made you stay?
>
> There was this one dude, leaned Lakers, but instead of dunking he actually asked me a
> real question about the bench matchup. It was weirdly disarming. Like, oh, we're
> actually talking now.
>
> How did that change things for you?
>
> I stopped trying to win and started just… talking ball. I even admitted our second unit
> is shaky, which I'd never do normally. A couple other people chilled out too after that.
>
> Did anything stand out about the other participants?
>
> That calm guy, almost too reasonable, part of me wondered if he was even a real fan,
> nobody's that level-headed on a Lakers-Celtics thread (laughs). But it worked.
>
> Did anything feel like common ground with the Lakers fans?
>
> Not the kumbaya stuff. When someone posted "we're all just NBA fans," I rolled my eyes.
> It was the prediction thing — we teamed up guessing the night's games and for once we
> were on the same side. That's when it stopped feeling like enemies.
>
> Did the way you think about Lakers fans shift at all?
>
> I mean, still rivals, obviously. But that one thread, yeah, they felt like people I'd
> actually hang out and watch a game with. Didn't expect that.

## 5. Level 3 — Theme + subthemes (→ results subsection)

**Theme: "Defusing is discovered, not declared."**
*Central organizing concept:* de-escalation took hold when a calmer interaction norm was
**modeled by a peer** and a common identity was **enacted through a shared task**, whereas
identity that was **explicitly stated** ("we're all NBA fans") was rejected as inauthentic.

- **Subtheme 3a — A single calm peer can reset a hostile norm.** One genuine,
  non-combative question shifted P7 from exiting to engaging, and the calmer tone spread.
- **Subtheme 3b — Discovered common identity is accepted; declared common identity is
  rejected.** The "we're all NBA fans" statement drew an eye-roll; the cooperative
  prediction task produced a felt "same side."
- **Subtheme 3c — Defusing preserves rivalry (dual identity).** Warmth toward the
  out-group coexisted with continued team identity — bias softened, not erased.

**Statement card (Miro):** *"It was the prediction thing — we teamed up guessing the
night's games and for once we were on the same side."* — P7 — title: *common identity via
shared task*.

**Example results paragraph (how it gets written):**
> Participants resisted explicit appeals to a shared identity but embraced one they
> enacted themselves. P7, a Celtics fan, "rolled [their] eyes" at a "we're all NBA fans"
> post, yet described the cooperative prediction task as the moment "it stopped feeling
> like enemies… for once we were on the same side" (**Subtheme 3b**). Notably, this
> warmth did not dissolve team loyalty — P7 remained "still rivals, obviously" while
> newly seeing Lakers fans as "people I'd watch a game with" (**Subtheme 3c**),
> consistent with a dual-identity rather than recategorization-by-erasure account.
